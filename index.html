The Architecture of Persuasion

Clarity versus Complexity in the AI Debate

By Izaik Tasker | ENGL 101 | Dec 2025

The Conflict

Throughout history, every major technology—from the printing press to the internet—has forced society to renegotiate how it is organized. Today, Artificial Intelligence is that force. But who controls the change? Optimistic innovators promising efficiency, or cautious regulators warning of harm?

My Argument: While regulatory warnings are necessary, they often fail to persuade because they are trapped in dense academic language. In contrast, corporate actors like McKinsey use clarity, optimism, and data to effectively shape the future.

Ethos Practical Authority vs. Academic Stacking

McKinsey builds credibility through "boots-on-the-ground" authority. By interviewing healthcare leaders, they signal that they know what is happening in the real world (Spatharou et al.).

In contrast, Zaidan and Ibrahim rely on "academic stacking"—piling up references to treaties and scholarships. While this works in a journal like Humanities & Social Sciences Communications, it feels distant to a general audience.

Pathos Hope vs. Abstract Fear

The McKinsey report (published by EIT Health and McKinsey) leans heavily into positive emotions. It frames AI not as a replacement, but as a relief for burned-out doctors.

"McKinsey makes readers feel emotionally invested in the solution; Zaidan and Ibrahim often make readers feel like observers of a distant, complicated problem."

Logos The Roadmap vs. The Maze

McKinsey builds a "logos of action." They quantify potential gains, creating a clear roadmap. Zaidan and Ibrahim build a "logos of complication," convincing the reader that the maze is too hard to solve.

Why Clarity Wins

In the AI debate, accessibility is not just a bonus; it is a core part of effective persuasion. Those who communicate with clarity and energy will shape the future. Those trapped in complex language will merely write about it after the fact.
